Coding - Competent Training Methods

It is a known fact that adults learn and are motivated differently than their adolescent counterparts. Academic studies have proven (time and time again) that a poor training process will produce poor learning outcomes. And as professional trainers of adults, it is Eleven Fifty's job to create value for its students. This means that they have to be good at what they do. Mastering training delivery is the key.

The importance of adult learning cannot be overestimated. A literate, educated, inquisitive, problem-solving workforce is essential for any company who wishes to maintain a leadership role in their respective marketplaces. The filed of adult learning encompasses the theory, research, concepts, assumptions, and guiding principles of educational experiences for adult learners. And Eleven Fifty Academy has found a way to deliver.

It is generally held that anyone can master most any task at a high level, if provided with high-quality instruction and time. The true difference between instructing and training is the production of an outcome. You can instruct someone to do something, but if it wasn't understood or well received, the trainee is likely not going to produce the outcome you were originally after. However, by training someone, you are demonstrating, and expect the trainee, in turn, to demonstrate the objectives. Every learning session is conducted by an expert with years of real-world experience. Learners follow the instruction in real time and reproduce the code blocks that are being taught, further reinforcing their learning experience.

Acquiring knowledge is not sufficient or effective for most learning. Learners need to make sense of what they learned nd know when, where, and how to use the knowledge. Understanding the information is, therfore, fundamental to effective learning.

Doing -- Learning is often for the practical purpose of developing competence in an activity. In most situations, learning involves doing the activities. Improvement in performance requires practice over time. In real-world situations, such as learning a coding language, involves all three of these learning components (knowledge, thinking, and doing). What's important here to understand is that all three of these components do not occur in a separate process, but are dynamic and mutually support the overall learning process. For example, as we acquire knowledge, think better about what we are doing, and practice a task, we tend to become more competent in that activity.

The trainers at Eleven Fifty Academy allow students to put the new skills they are learining to work. In fact - it is absolutely imperative that they do - before they forget it. There are many theories for why people forget things. The most improtant for coding instructors is that over 60% of factual information will be lost within 48 hours if there is no subsequent rehearsal or review of what was learned. Skills and understanding are much more resistant to forgetting. For example, if you consider the adage about riding a bike - once you learn how to ride a bike - you never forget. The more information is reviewed in the first day or so after it is learned, the more likely the chance of it becoming established in long term memory.
